# Doorslam-no-Jutsu and Human Memory

*On memory, surprise, and why the stories we retell the most are the ones we get the most wrong.*

---

This repository holds a short essay and its research-facing companion. Both grew out of a single conversation that started as a trivial argument about a twenty-year-old manga memory and ended somewhere unexpected: a working account of **why human memory keeps some things and quietly rewrites others** — and how that same principle turns up, coming the other way, in the design of an artificial mind.

The essay, **[*The Jutsu Lost to Time*](https://mushroomfleet.substack.com/p/the-jutsu-lost-to-time)**, is written for a general reader in the tradition of Asimov's science essays: translate the jargon, flag the fence where settled science ends, and never hide the cracks. The grounding artifact is the technical record underneath it — the reasoning journey, the claims marked as *established* or *extension*, and the open questions — written for anyone who wants to develop or test the idea.

## What this is for

The project is a small case study in how memory actually works, built around a memory that turned out to be **beautifully, instructively wrong.** A friend recalled the exact moment a beloved comic's hero began his fall — a slammed door, a fan-nicknamed "doorslam-no-jutsu," *"the most powerful jutsu, lost to time."* When the original pages were finally checked, the large shape of the memory was correct and the surface had quietly drifted: the door burst *open*, not shut; the blundering character was a look-alike wearing another man's name.

That gap — between the durable meaning and the flowing surface — is the subject. The work assembles it from three established ideas and one live one:

- **Reconstruction, not playback** — Bartlett's *War of the Ghosts* (1932): remembering rebuilds, and rebuilding edits toward expectation.
- **Gist vs. verbatim** — fuzzy-trace theory (Brainerd & Reyna): two traces stored in parallel, the surface fading faster than the meaning.
- **Surprisal, not importance** — prediction-error accounts of encoding: what a prior could never have guessed is what earns high-fidelity storage.
- **Reconsolidation** — Nader et al. (2000): retrieval reopens a memory for editing, so the stories we retell most are the ones most exposed to drift.

And then the turn: the same governing quantity — **surprise** — is what a purpose-built *laughter* module (from the author's own work on artificial minds) exists to detect. A laugh is the resolution of a *held incompatibility*: the instant a confident prior breaks and reforms into something better. Human memory and that module agree on what deserves attention and then make **opposite decisions about what to store** — and the essay argues that a single mind may need both.

## The corrected passage

The one load-bearing detail that needed fixing was the "look-alike" claim. The essay first treated the two characters as separable by a single feature — the colour of a pair of glasses the reader never saw in black and white. That overstated the case, and the fix turned the objection into a second witness for the thesis:

> These two are the kind of characters memory was built to blur: interchangeable background jonin, wheeled into scenes to react and rarely to speak, and this was the one time it mattered which was which. Everything about them a prior could predict — the forehead protector, the flak vest — my mind sensibly declined to store, trusting it could refill the standard-issue details on demand. What actually told them apart was finer: Ebisu's round lenses against Aoba's wrap-around shades, and the red of Aoba's frames. The shapes were on the page, in black and white, and memory smoothed them to a flat "two men in sunglasses" anyway. The colour would have been the clean giveaway — and colour was the one thing the greyscale page could never hand me. The predictable, it threw away; the unpredictable, it was never given. Two roads to the same forgetting.

The predictable cues were discarded because a prior could refill them for free; the one unpredictable cue was withheld by the medium itself. Both roads lead to the same forgetting — which is the essay's whole argument, running in miniature.
