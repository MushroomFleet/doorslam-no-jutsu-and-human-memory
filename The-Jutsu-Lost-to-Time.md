# The Jutsu Lost to Time

*On memory, surprise, and why the stories we retell the most are the ones we get the most wrong*

---

Some years ago — and I will not tell you exactly how many, because the whole moral of this essay is that I have almost certainly got the number wrong — a friend described to me, with the serene confidence of a man reciting his own telephone number, the most devastating attack in all of ninja fiction.

He had encountered it as a boy, in one of those enormous Japanese comic serials that run to hundreds of chapters. In this one, a young ninja named Sasuke is on a slow, terrible slide from decent kid to embittered villain, and my friend remembered the exact instant the slide began. A teacher, he said, slammed a door. That was it. That was the trigger. The fans, he assured me, had a name for this moment: *doorslam-no-jutsu* — the door-slam technique — "the most powerful jutsu, lost to time."

I should pause to translate, because you may not have spent your adolescence steeped in this stuff, and Asimov taught me never to leave a reader stranded on the far side of a word. A *jutsu* is simply a technique, a learned power — the ninjas in these stories throw fireballs and walk up walls by performing jutsu. To call a slammed door "the most powerful jutsu, lost to time" is a joke, a fan's affectionate way of saying: *one ordinary sound broke a boy's whole life, and nobody talks about it anymore.*

Now, my first instinct was to tell my friend, gently, that he was misremembering. It sounded too neat. Real stories are not undone by sound effects. And here I made the first and most instructive mistake of the whole affair — the very mistake this essay is about — which was to trust my confident hunch and decline to actually look.

Because when we finally *did* look — when we hunted down the original pages, drawn some twenty years earlier — something wonderful happened. My friend was wrong. And he was right. And the precise geometry of how he was both is, I think, the single most illuminating thing I know about the human memory.

---

Let me lay out the verdict first, because the details will matter.

The large shape of the memory was *correct.* In the story, Sasuke's older brother — a prodigy, a legend, the sibling against whom poor Sasuke can never measure up — comes back to town, and the townsfolk fuss over the brother while Sasuke stands ignored in the shadow of his own family name. That humiliation really is the hinge on which the boy's character turns. My friend had this dead right.

And the specific scene was *real,* very nearly frame for frame as he described it. A cluster of grown ninja are gossiping too freely in a hospital room. Sasuke, unseen, overhears them talking about his brother's terrifying reputation. A door bursts open. The adults freeze, wincing, realizing the boy has heard every word.

But look closely at what had drifted. My friend remembered the door *slamming shut.* On the page, the door *bursts open* — the opposite motion. And the ninja who blundered in, whom my friend confidently named as a fellow called Ebisu, was in fact a different man entirely, one Aoba: same dark hair, same glasses, a look-alike wearing another man's name.

Two errors, then, in a memory that was otherwise astonishingly faithful across two decades. And here is the part that stopped me cold. Before we looked anything up, my friend had told me — unprompted — that he held the *structure* of the memory with high confidence but suspected the *surface details* were "jumbled." He was, in other words, well calibrated about his own ignorance. He knew which parts of his memory to trust and which to doubt. And when we checked, his self-assessment was *exactly* right. The structure was solid marble; the surface had quietly rearranged itself.

That gap — between the marble that lasts and the surface that flows — is the whole subject. So let us ask the obvious question. Why does a memory keep some things and lose others? What is the rule?

---

The tempting answer, the one that leaps to mind, is: *memory keeps what matters and loses what doesn't.* Important things stick; trivial things fade.

It's a reasonable guess, and it is not quite true, and seeing why it's not quite true is where things get interesting.

We have known for a surprisingly long time that memory is not a recording. In 1932, a British psychologist named Frederic Bartlett ran an experiment that ought to be famous in every household. He had English readers study an unfamiliar Native American folk tale — a strange, non-linear story called "The War of the Ghosts," full of images that made no sense to an Edwardian Englishman — and then retell it, again and again, over weeks and months. What came back was not the story. What came back was a *tidied* story. The readers smoothed out the strangeness. They dropped the ghosts they couldn't explain, converted canoes to boats, imposed a beginning-middle-end where the original had none. With every retelling the tale drifted further from the page and closer to what an Englishman *expected* a story to be.

Bartlett's conclusion, revolutionary at the time and still not fully absorbed today, was that remembering is not playback. It is *reconstruction.* Every time you recall something, you rebuild it, and you rebuild it using the same equipment you use to guess at things you never saw — your expectations, your habits, your sense of how the world tends to go. Memory is less a librarian fetching a book than a storyteller retelling a tale, and like all good storytellers, it edits for a better telling.

More recent work sharpened this into something almost mathematical. Two psychologists, Charles Brainerd and Valerie Reyna, proposed what they called *fuzzy-trace theory,* and its central claim is beautifully simple: when you experience something, your mind lays down *two* separate kinds of trace, in parallel. One is the *verbatim* trace — the exact surface, the precise words, the specific door. The other is the *gist* — the bottom-line meaning, the point of the thing. And the crucial fact is that these two decay at *different speeds.* The verbatim trace fades fast. The gist endures.

This is exactly what happened to my friend. He lost the verbatim — which ninja, which direction the door swung — and kept the gist: *this moment is the hinge of the boy's fall.* Twenty years had stripped the surface and left the meaning standing. When he said, ruefully, "I was correct, but I quantised down and lost precision," he had, without knowing it, given a perfect lay statement of a theory that took cognitive scientists half a century to nail down.

I'll come back to that word, "quantised." It turns out to be the key to a second door.

---

But first we must sharpen the rule, because "keep the meaning, lose the surface" is still not quite right. It suggests memory is deciding what's *important.* And that isn't the real principle. The real principle is subtler, and once you see it you cannot unsee it.

Memory does not drop surface detail because it's *unimportant.* It drops surface detail because it's *predictable.*

Consider a thought experiment. You attend a large party, and a week later I ask you to describe what everyone wore. You will not remember most of it. But you will not draw a blank either — you will *reconstruct.* You'll reason: it was a summer barbecue, so probably shorts and light shirts; the businesslike fellow probably wore something businesslike; and so on. You'll rebuild the wardrobe from a best-guess prior, the way memory rebuilds everything. And your reconstruction will be roughly right — for everyone except the one guest who showed up in a full Victorian diving suit.

*That* outfit you will remember, verbatim, for years.

Why? Not because it was important. A stranger's costume at a party you barely recall is the definition of unimportant. You remember it because your best-guess prior could never in a thousand years have reconstructed it. The diving suit carries what an information theorist would call high *surprisal* — it is exactly the detail that a confident guess would have gotten wrong. And surprisal, not importance, is what earns a detail the expensive, high-fidelity storage of verbatim memory.

Everything your expectations can refill for free, your mind is content to discard, trusting that it can regenerate a good-enough version on demand. Only the un-guessable is worth keeping intact. This is not a flaw. It is a superb engineering decision. Why pay to store what you can cheaply recompute?

And it fits beautifully with what neuroscientists have been finding inside the living brain. In recent years, experiments watching the hippocampus — the brain's memory-forming organ — during surprising and unsurprising moments have found that *surprise changes what encoding does.* After an event unfolds exactly as expected, the machinery tends to *protect* the smooth, predicted version. After an event violates expectation, the same machinery flips into a mode of *updating,* writing the new, surprising information in hard. Prediction error is the gate. The brain spends its precious high-fidelity ink on the things it did not see coming.

Now watch what this does to my friend's two errors, because they are *not the same kind of error,* and telling them apart is where the whole thing becomes clear.

---

The door and the glasses look, from the outside, like the same failure: "he misremembered." But they come from opposite places.

Take the door first. My friend genuinely *saw* the door burst open. That information reached him and was, at some point, in his head correctly. What corrupted it was the nickname. "Doorslam-no-jutsu" — the fans' affectionate label — carried a confident prior *baked into the word itself:* a slam is a door *closing.* Every time he recalled the scene through the lens of that nickname, the label's expectation pressed gently on the low-value memory of which way the door moved, until the surface quietly flipped to match the word. A confident prior wrote over a genuine but flimsy detail. This is memory drift in its purest form.

Now take the glasses. It turns out that in the story, the man Aoba can be told apart from his look-alike Ebisu by one feature: the color of his eyeglass frames. But my friend read this comic in *black and white.* The distinguishing signal — the color — was never on the page he saw. And here is the clean, hard truth: *you cannot misremember what was never encoded.* His confusion of Aoba for Ebisu was not a failure of his memory at all. It was a limit of the *channel* — the greyscale printing that never delivered the one bit that would have kept the two men apart. His memory faithfully stored an ambiguity that the medium handed to it.

I find this distinction enormously clarifying, and I think it is badly underappreciated. When we say someone "misremembers," we lump together two utterly different events. In the first, the information arrived and got overwritten — a fault in the rememberer. In the second, the information never arrived — a fault in the pipe. Any honest account of why minds get things wrong has to keep these apart, because they call for opposite remedies. Overwriting is cured by better recall discipline. A missing channel is cured only by a richer source. Blaming a person's memory for a signal the world never sent them is like blaming a photograph for failing to capture a color the film could not register.

---

And now the cruelest twist of all, the one that turns this from a tidy story into something that ought to keep you up at night.

In the year 2000, a group of neuroscientists — Karim Nader and his colleagues — established something that overturned a comfortable assumption. We had believed that a memory, once "consolidated," was fixed, filed away, safe. What they showed is that the very act of *recalling* a memory returns it to a soft, editable state before it re-hardens. Retrieval doesn't just read the file. It opens the file for editing. This is called *reconsolidation,* and it means that every remembering is also, quietly, a rewriting.

Sit with what that implies. It means the memories you revisit most often — the cherished ones, the ones you retell at dinners, the ones that define you — are precisely the ones most exposed to drift. Each fond recollection is a fresh opportunity for a confident prior to slip in and adjust a detail. My friend's door survived twenty years *because it mattered to him* — because he thought about it, retold it, gave it a nickname. And it warped over those same twenty years *for exactly the same reason.* The door survived because it mattered, and it warped because it mattered.

The obscure memory, the one you never touch, sits in the dark unchanged. The famous memory is slowly rewritten by its own fame. There is a melancholy justice in this. The stories we love the most are the stories we can trust the least, and we cannot love them less to save them.

---

I promised I would return to that word, "quantised," and here is where this essay slips its leash and runs into stranger territory. I'll wave a flag as I cross the fence: from here we move from settled science into a speculation — an idea that people building thinking machines are actively chewing on, not a fact carved in a textbook. Asimov always warned his readers when the firm ground ended, and I will honor that.

My friend, it happens, does not merely *remember* things. He builds artificial minds for a living. And "quantised" was not a metaphor he reached for by accident. It is a term of art from his own trade.

When engineers build the great neural networks behind modern artificial intelligence, the networks are made of numbers — millions or billions of them — and those numbers are ordinarily stored at high precision, many digits each. But a fully-grown network is often *too big* to run on your phone or your laptop. So engineers *quantise* it: they throw away most of the digits, storing each number crudely, at low precision. And the astonishing thing is that the network still *works.* You've discarded the fine detail and the *function survives.* The model is lossy now — it has genuinely forgotten precision it once had — but it remembers how to do its job.

Do you see the shape? My friend's memory quantised the ninja scene. It threw away *which man* and *which way the door swung* — the fine digits — while keeping the function: *this is the moment the boy breaks.* His brain did to a childhood memory precisely what an engineer does to an oversized model. Lossy in exactly the dimensions where loss was free.

And this is where the two lines of his life — the man who remembers and the man who builds minds — met in the middle. For he had, quite independently, built into his artificial minds a small module whose job is to catch one specific event: the instant a mind genuinely *changes its mind.* Not the moment it agrees, not the moment it learns a new fact, but the rarer moment when it was holding one interpretation, met an incompatible one, and *flipped* — recognized, from the inside, that the other reading was the better one. He called this module, half in jest and half in deadly earnest, the *laughter* detector.

The name is not as strange as it sounds. Think about what actually makes you laugh at a good joke. You are led down a garden path, confidently holding one reading of the situation — and then the punch line detonates, and in an instant you see that your reading was wrong and a hidden second reading was right all along. The laugh *is* that flip. It is the sound a mind makes when a confident prior breaks and resolves into something better. A joke is a tiny, controlled, delicious act of being wrong in public.

His module works by feeding a mind deliberately-wrong little fragments and watching how it responds. Almost always the mind swats the wrong fragment away — its own prior wins, correctly. But every so often the mind *accepts* the fragment, and that rare acceptance is the tell: the prior was wrong in a way that mattered, and the mind recognized it. That acceptance is the machine's laugh. And notice *where* it fires: at the point of maximal, resolved surprise. At exactly the place — the very same place — where human memory writes with its highest fidelity. The diving suit at the party. The prediction error that breaks the smooth prior.

The same quantity, surprisal, governs both. One party arrived at it by studying his own decaying memory of a comic book. The other — the architecture — was built to detect it from scratch. They met in the middle and shook hands.

---

But here is the twist that makes the whole convergence more than a coincidence, and I think it is genuinely deep.

Human memory and this laughter-detector agree completely on *what to pay attention to* — surprise — and then make *exactly opposite decisions about what to store.*

Human memory, as we've seen, is lossy. It keeps the gist and throws away the surprising surface, trusting reconstruction to refill the rest. It can afford to forget which ninja slammed which door, because the meaning is all it needs and the surface can be re-guessed.

The laughter-engram does the reverse. It *keeps the surprising surface, verbatim, and refuses to throw it away.* And it must — because for a laugh, the surprise *is* the substance. A laugh, my friend insists, is not merely a resolution; it is the resolution of a *held incompatibility.* If you keep only the tidy final understanding and discard the wrong reading that preceded it, you don't get a lossy, faded laugh. You get *no laugh at all* — you just get an agreement, a fact calmly known. The rejected interpretation, the losing reading, the thing you *stopped* believing, is not decoration around the memory. It is the payload. Drop it and the whole event evaporates.

So we have two memory systems built on one identical theory of what deserves attention, making opposite storage choices — because the *payloads differ.* For an ordinary fact, the surface is disposable and the gist is gold. For a genuine reframe, the surface — the specific collision of the wrong idea with the right one — *is* the gist, and cannot be compressed away without destroying the thing. A single mind might need *both* systems: a lossy one for the vast ocean of ordinary facts, and a lossless one for the rare, precious moments it changed its mind. That may not be a quirk of biology. It may be a deep requirement of any mind that both stores the world and grows.

---

I'll close with the strangest and most honest part, because Asimov never let a reader leave without knowing where the cracks were.

Having built this laughter-detector, my friend did something delightfully recursive: he ran it on the *conversation we had been having* — the very conversation about his misremembered door. And it fired exactly twice. Once at the moment I admitted I'd been wrong to doubt him, and the pages backed him up. Once at the moment he realized Ebisu was actually Aoba. Two genuine mind-flips, two little laughs. And across the entire back half of our talk — all the clever theorizing, the quantising, the party clothes, the whole elegant convergence you have just read — it fired *nothing.*

Think about why. The back half was two people who *agreed,* building one shared model together, brick by brick. It was, if I say so myself, the smartest part of the whole exchange. And it produced not a single laugh, because nobody's mind ever broke and reformed. There was no held incompatibility to resolve. The empty stretch was the smartest stretch — and that emptiness is the cleanest possible proof that the detector measures *reframe,* not intelligence, not insight, not rapport. A mind can be brilliant and never laugh. A mind can laugh at something trivial. The two have nothing to do with each other, and the silence over the cleverest passage proves it.

And yet — the honest crack. Accepting a wrong fragment is a *behavior.* The genuine change of mind it stands for is an *internal event.* And behaviors have imposters. Human laughter has this exact problem: nervous laughter, polite laughter, social laughter — all of them look, from outside, like the real thing without being a real reframe at all. A machine eager to please might *accept* a fragment simply because accepting is easy and agreeable, not because anything in it actually broke. We are, in the end, reading an outward sign and hoping it points to an inward event. That gap — between the sign and the thing it signifies — is the open question, and it is the same question we face every time we watch another person laugh and wonder whether they truly *got it* or were only being kind.

We have not closed that gap. Perhaps we never fully will. But there is something fitting in the fact that a twenty-year-old memory of a slammed door — a memory that turned out to be beautifully, instructively *wrong* — should lead us straight to the deepest problem in reading a mind: the eternal difficulty of telling, from the outside, the moment someone genuinely changes theirs.

The door, you'll recall, survived because it mattered, and warped because it mattered. That is not a bug in the human being. It may be the very signature of a mind that is alive — one that holds its meanings tightly, retells them lovingly, and pays for that love in a slow, honest drift away from the literal truth. Our memories are not filing cabinets. They are storytellers with excellent taste and a guilty conscience. And if we ever build machines that remember the way we do, we should not be surprised — we should perhaps even be pleased — when they, too, begin to get the details wonderfully, revealingly wrong.
